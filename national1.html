The Vaping Epidemic: A Closer Look at the Smoking Landscape of the Philippines

The Philippines, just like many other countries grapple with the issues of tobacco smoking and the rise of electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes) as alternative smoking systems particularly to the Filipino youth. This prompted nation leaders to actively initiate discussions, policies, and implementations in the 10th Session of the Conference of the Parties (COP10) to regulate the growing issue.

Tobacco smoking has long since been a standing issue of the country with factors that include easy accessibility and targeted marketing by cigar companies. Despite the existing control laws and regulations such as the nationwide imposition of higher tobacco taxes that brought exemplary results, government officials still highlight the room for improvement in navigating the smoking scheme of the country.

In recent events, the growing presence of vapes and e-cigarettes provide a new dimension to the smoking landscape of the Philippines, particularly to the youth as data cited from the 2019 Global Youth Tobacco Survey show an alarming rise in vape usage among teenagers aged 13-15, translating to millions of young Filipinos despite a related prohibition in the Republic Act 11900 or the Vape Regulation Law that states the age access of vapes is limited at 18 years old.

The appeal of vape and e-cigarettes root from easy accessibility such as online shops, varied flavors, and the perceived lighter health risks compared to tobacco smoking, however, the World Health Organization (WHO) says otherwise as they stress the health dangers of such smoking devices containing a highly addictive chemical, nicotine and its long-term effects on the body such as heart and lung diseases.

“The delegation should speak rather than hide, whitewash, or disguise the truth of the serious threat to public health brought about by weak Philippine regulation on ecigarettes,” the joint statement of former health and education officials said.

In response to the pressing issues, government agencies such as the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI) actively intensify monitoring and enforcement operations against offenders of the Republic Act 11900. They have reported to have confiscated over 18,000 non-compliant vape products valued at approximately P5.5 million at both physical and online shops to further bar minors from accessing vapes and e-cigarettes in addition to issuing strict certifications and registrations.

The growing concern on the rise of vape and e-cigarette smoking to among the Filipino youth continues to be a standing struggle in the country as monitoring and regulations in the country exhibit room for improvement that can only be responded with the constant collaboration of the government agencies through pro-active discussions, policymaking, and monitoring of the smoking landscape of the country to make air a little cleaner for the future generations.
